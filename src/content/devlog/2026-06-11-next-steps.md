In 2012 (when the Mayans ended the world), I was in my dorm room thinking of a name for the hypothetical studio where I would publish all of my games. I had come to like "Barely Conscious Games" because it sort of had a double meaning to me. See, a couple years into college I really started paying attention to this foggy feeling in my mind that made it difficult to concentrate; it felt like I was _barely_ conscious all the time. Turns out it was ADHD, but it took another decade for me to piece that mystery together.

So anyway, the next 14 years were spent making games (mostly [Stonequest](https://www.barelyconscious.games/stonequest)) off and on in my spare time, between college, then internships, then a career. At some point, the career took over everything and I stopped considering myself someone who makes games. 

And then one day in spring 2024, everything changed.

### That one day in spring 2024 when everything changed

It was the week after spring break, my niece and nephew went back to school, and I had taken an extra week off work to do whatever I wanted. So I decided to do a two-day [game jam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_jam). The theme was "technology cats", and by the end of these two days, I actually had a playable game in the style of a very simple creature collector.

I learned a lot from this experience, technically speaking, but the main thing I learned was that _I could do it_. It had been years since I made anything meaningful and I thought I had lost the ability along the way - that corporate SWE had drained me of any passion. This bit of inspiration was the spark that ultimately led to me quitting my job after 8 years.

### What's Next?

At my last job, there was this thing called a _DFAD_, or a _date-for-a-date_. If someone asked when you were going to be done with something, you could instead say "_I don't know, ask me again on June 18th_" and that was a perfectly valid thing to say.

![Not for another 2 hours](/devlog/next-steps/guess.png)

Sometimes a DFAD would turn into a DFADFAD  (date-for-a-date-for-a-date). Hypothetically, this can be repeated forever, or until they finally stop asking. In practice, you really only get max 2 or 3 before your manager asks you why they're being asked why you're not giving a date. So now you have _two_ different people bugging you about this thing. The way I solved this was with a _roadmap_: a glorious spreadsheet filled with dates and action items.

### The Road to Next Fest

4 days from now, [Steam Next Fest](https://store.steampowered.com/sale/nextfest) is starting, which is a great and terrifying opportunity for an indie developer to showcase their game, get some visibility, and gather feedback from players. A successful Next Fest can really put you on the map, but a bad demo - or worse, one that doesn't work - would be a significant setback. There's a whole lot left to do in Script Kitties and 4 days isn't a lot of time. 

I'm gonna be honest buds, it ain't lookin good. It's not gonna happen in 4 days.

Alright, so 2026 is out of the question, what about 2027? Theoretically, Steam will still exist and if they continue hosting Next Fest, then that means I have about 369 days to write the story, hire an artist, build out a steam page, and test the heck out of it to make sure it doesn't crash. 

That's doable, right?
